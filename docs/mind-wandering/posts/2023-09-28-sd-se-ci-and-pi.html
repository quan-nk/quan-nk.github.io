SD, SE, CI and PI

Uncategorized
Published

28 tháng 9, 2023

Standard Error (SE) and Standard Deviation (SD) are two statistical concepts that often cause confusion. These also lead to misconception in two relevant statistics: Confidence interval, and Prediction interval.

Before diving in, please make sure you (1) understood what is the mean of a dataset and (2) are aware of the difference between a sample and a population.

Let’s say there are nine billions of people in this world, of which 1.2 billion are adolescent. We are interested in the prevalence of depression among adolescent, but we cannot define the exact number of adolescents who has/do not have depression. Thus, we conduct a much-smaller observational study among 1,000 children in Vietnam. We aim to use data from this 1000-children dataset to generate an estimate for the global prevalence of depression among 1.2 billion adolescents that will always be hidden (no one can reach to and conduct a research on 1.2 billion adolescents, can’t they?).

In the scenario, the sample would include 1,000 children (and we can have a dataset of this); while the population of interested was estimated to be around 1.2 billion.

Now, let’s get the ball rolling with four stats: SD, SEM, 95% CI, and 95% PI.

1. Standard Deviation (SD)

Let’s imaging we display every single value of the dataset (of a sample) into a plot.

SD measures the variability (or the spread) of data points around the mean in a given dataset. In other words, it tells us, on average, how far each data point is away from the mean. But as I said, there are two concepts: sample and population. Let’s explore.

SD of the population

If we have ALL the data points from a population (1,2 billion of adolescent as in our prime example, well, it sounds impossible), we can compute the value of the population SD using the following formula:

\(latex \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (X - \mu)^2}{N}} &s=3\)

\(latex \sigma =&s=2\) Population SD

\(latex \mu = &s=2\) Population Mean

\(latex N = &s=2\) Total number of observations in Population (well, in this example it is 1,2 billion!)

This is just the formula. It is impossible to calculate this number in real world (time constraints, financial or technical limitations), hence, a sample comes up to provide an (measurable) estimate of this \(latex \mu&s=2\) utilizing data from a subset.

Tips: in Stats, they prefer using ‘complicating’ Greek symbols like \(latex \mu, \tau, \alpha, \beta , ... &s=2\) for unknown variables that we can barely measure in real life.

SD of the sample

By collecting random samples (e.g. 1,000 adolescents) from the population, we can calculate the** Sample SD, and then make inferences about the population standard deviation using this Sample SD**.

\(latex S = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (X - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}} &s=3\)

\(latex S =&s=2\) Sample SD

\(latex \bar{x} = &s=2\) Sample Mean

\(latex n = &s=2\) Total number of observations in the sample (\(latex = 1000 &s=2\))

In this case , \(latex df = n-1 &s=2\) is called the degree of freedom (nevermind, it’s out of scope).

2. Standard Error (SE)

Tips: Let’s explore the difference between sample distribution and sampling distribution. SE is relevant to the sampling distribution.

The Standard Error measures how far the sample statistic (e.g., sample mean) is likely to be from the true population statistic (e.g., the population mean), see image below. There are two major related concepts: Standard Error Mean (SEM) and Standard Error Proportion (SEP). Let’s just talk about SEM here for brevity but please do look out other statistics afterwards.

\(latex SEM = \frac{S}{\sqrt{n}} &s=3\)

\(latex S = &s=2\) Sample SD

\(latex n = &s=2\) Total number of observations in the Sample

3. 95% Confidence interval (95% CI) and 95% Prediction interval (95% PI)

At this point, you just need the definitions and equations of CI and PI to know what they really are.

Confidence interval pertains to a statistic estimated from multiple values. It expresses sampling uncertainty.

Prediction interval expresses inherent uncertainty in the particular data point on top of the sampling uncertainty. A prediction interval is a statistical concept used in regression analysis and forecasting to estimate a range of likely values for a future observation or outcome. In statistical inference, specifically predictive inference, a prediction interval is an estimate of an interval in which a future observation will fall, with a certain probability, given what has already been observed.
